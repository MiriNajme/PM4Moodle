## Interviews' Summaries

**Note 1:** All modules' and users' ratings by the interviewees are on a scale from 1 to 7, where 7 is the most important.

**Note 2:** Modules or user-related elements not assigned a rating were either not used by the participant or not considered important enough to prioritize.

---

### Interview summary (I1)

**Participant profile:**  
Experienced instructor and regular Moodle user with moderate self-rated familiarity with the platform (5/7).

**Main analytical interests:**  
The participant was mainly interested in group-level rather than individual-level analysis, explicitly stating: *“I’m not very interested in a particular individual,”* and that they need to act in groups of 30–40. The most valuable insights were general trends about what groups of students do, especially whether students had actually read the posted materials or listened to the recorded lectures before related exercises. Such information was seen as useful for concrete teaching actions, such as reminding students to watch a lecture before the next exercise.

The participant supported cross-course comparison to obtain *“an overview”* of engagement, teaching styles, and reliance on Moodle, including whether some courses are more or less “Moodle intensive.” At the same time, the participant warned against drawing overly strong conclusions due to differences in teaching approaches. Beyond student activity, the participant wanted insight into teacher activity, for example *“how often are they active in a consistent fashion?”* File- and video-related use was also highlighted, such as how many students viewed a file and *“how many minutes of a certain video have been viewed.”*

**User importance:**  
- Teacher (7)  
- Student (7)  
- Group (6)

Group-level visibility was seen as useful to understand *“who in the group does what.”* The participant emphasized that more data are only useful if they can be acted upon and noted they already have *“too much data.”*

**Module importance:**  
- Assignment (7)  
- File (7)  
- Forum (6)  
- Chat (6)  
- URL (6)  
- Quiz (5)  
- Page (5)  
- Folder (2)  
- Choice (1)

### Interview summary (I2)

**Participant profile:**  
Experienced instructor and regular Moodle user with moderate self-rated familiarity with the platform (5/7).

**Main analytical interests:**  
The participant was interested in understanding how much students use the system and materials, how they communicate, and how they progress through the course—*“how much students are engaged,”* *“how much they interact with the system,”* and *“in which order they use the different parts.”* For example, whether quizzes are taken immediately after downloading materials or later, closer to the exam.

A further interest concerned how students experience learning materials, especially identifying difficult parts that require additional support. Student progression—*“when they learn”* and *“how much time it takes to learn”*—was also emphasized as support for better course design. Cross-course comparison was seen as useful for comparing teaching designs and learning approaches. Personalization was described as *“the toughest yet most interesting aspect.”* Unused features such as feedback and surveys were mentioned as potentially valuable if applied effectively.

**User importance:**  
- Student (7)  
- Teacher (7)  
- Manager (5)  
- Group (3)

**Module importance:**  
- Forum (7)  
- Assignment (7)  
- File (7)  
- Folder (7)  
- Page (7)  
- Label (7)  
- URL (7)  
- Wiki (5)  
- Quiz (4)

The participant stressed that Moodle data alone cannot fully capture student behavior, as students may use external tools or communicate outside the platform.

### Interview summary (I3)

**Participant profile:**  
Regular Moodle user with limited-to-moderate self-rated familiarity (3–4/7). Use was mainly task-focused, such as handling specific modules, assessing group projects, and uploading materials.

**Main analytical interests:**  
The participant was interested in whether engagement in formative learning activities is associated with later assessment success, particularly whether students completing non-mandatory tests perform better in final exams.

Another concern was identifying weaker students within group work, since a group may submit successfully even when some members struggle. Cross-course comparison was seen as useful for comparing teaching strategies and formats. The participant also expressed strong interest in how students access materials, video watching patterns, and whether some parts are repeatedly viewed or abandoned early.

**User importance:**  
Described as *“vital”* (no numerical ratings):  
- Manager  
- Student  
- Teacher  
- Non-editing teacher  
- Course creator

**Module importance:**  
- Assignment (7)  
- File (7)  
- Forum (6)  
- URL (6)  
- External tool (5)

Quizzes were emphasized as very important but not rated due to limited direct use. The participant stressed the need for system integration beyond Moodle.

### Interview summary (I4)

**Participant profile:**  
Highly experienced instructor and expert Moodle user with several years of experience.

**Main analytical interests:**  
The participant focused on whether students actually read or used provided materials and how this reflects the effectiveness of teaching efforts. Differences in engagement, submissions, attendance, and participation across groups and formats were seen as critical for improving design and resource use.

Cross-course and process-oriented analyses were supported, including forum usage, teacher workload, inefficient practices, feedback routines, and peer review. The participant emphasized understanding frequency and duration of interactions to support decisions about course design and resource allocation.

**User importance:**  
- Manager (7)  
- Course creator (7, when also manager)  
- Teacher (5)  
- Student (important)  
- Non-editing teacher (least important)

**Module importance:**  
- File (7)  
- Folder (7)  
- Page (7)  
- URL (7)  
- Forum (7)  
- Assignment (7)  
- Quiz (7)  
- Workshop (5)

Survey and feedback modules were generally not used. System complexity and usability were highlighted as major constraints.

### Interview summary (I5)

**Participant profile:**  
Experienced instructor with moderate-to-high familiarity (5–6/7).

**Main analytical interests:**  
The participant was especially interested in quiz analytics, such as relationships among number of attempts and time spent, noting that different patterns may indicate guessing, careful work, or cheating. Complementary inquiry (e.g., student interviews) was seen as necessary.

File and URL access was considered useful but ambiguous. Cross-course analysis was valued for understanding workload distribution. Quiz and assignment data were seen as potentially useful for exploring correlations with exam performance, though strong conclusions were cautioned against.

**User importance:**  
- Student (7)  
- Teacher (6)  
- Manager (6)  
- Non-editing teacher (4–5)  
- Guest (lowest priority)  
- Course creator (low priority)  
- Authenticated user on front page (low priority)

**Module importance:**  
- Assignment (7)  
- Quiz (7)  
- File (7)  
- Forum (6)  
- Choice (6)  
- URL (6)  
- Feedback (5)

Analytics were seen as indicative rather than definitive.

### Interview summary (I6)

**Participant profile:**  
Experienced instructor with high familiarity (6/7), using the system for 6–7 years.

**Main analytical interests:**  
The participant wanted analytics beyond access counts, including sentiment analysis of forum posts and time-based engagement metrics. Assignment resubmissions were highlighted as signals of uncertainty or misunderstanding.

The participant stressed that logs often require contextual follow-up and should not be interpreted in isolation.

**User importance:**  
- Student (most important)

**Module importance:**  
- File (7)  
- URL (7)  
- Forum (7)  
- Assignment (7)  
- Choice (7)  
- Quiz (6)

Analytics were viewed as a way to identify situations worth investigating, not to make assumptions.

**Participant profile:**  
Highly experienced educator with limited-to-moderate recent familiarity (3/7).

**Main analytical interests:**

**User importance:**
